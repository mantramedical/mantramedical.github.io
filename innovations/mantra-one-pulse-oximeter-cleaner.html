Mantra One® — Pulse oximeter cleaner European patent number: 2595561

The Mantra One®, our pulse oximeter cleaning device, uses patented automation to deliver consistent cleaning of pulse oximeters at the bedside.

Our innovation overcomes the limitations of current cleaning methods by integrating into existing workflow, providing effective cleaning of pulse oximeters without adding to staff workload.

How it works

The Mantra One® ensures pulse oximeters are cleaned before and after each patient, with a predictable and reliable cleaning action at minimal financial and environmental cost.

  1. When the pulse oximeter needs to be used, it is removed from the  Mantra One® and placed on the patient's finger.

  2. The Mantra One® automatically prepares a fresh cleaning surface, ready for re-docking of the pulse oximeter.

  3. After removal from the patient, the pulse oximeter is re-docked back with the Mantra One® and cleaning starts immediately.

  4. The pulse oximeter will be clean and ready for the next patient.

The device retrofits onto existing wheeled observation trolleys enabling cleaning at the bedside. Physical cleaning is delivered via a replaceable cartridge housed within the device.

Perfomance vs existing cleaning methods

In summary, our innovation will:

  • provide fast, automated bedside cleaning of pulse oximeters
  • work within existing staff workflow pathways
  • provide a combined physical cleaning and storage function
  • deliver a consistent and reproducible cleaning action
  • present a fresh cleaning surface for every use
  • rapid cleaning allows use between each patient on a ward round
  • provide at least a 4-log microorganism kill-rate
  • be cost-effective and environmentally friendly

Why is cleaning pulse oximeters a problem?

Pulse oximeters are recognised as important vectors of HCAI spread1-2.
The World Health Organisation's Saves Lives report stated that:

…implementation of standard precautions for all patients all the time is key to preventing spread of all microorganisms…

What is a pulse oximeter?

The pulse oximeter is one of the most common pieces of medical equipment in the world and is considered standard equipment for almost all secondary care patient interactions.

Because the pulse oximeter measures both pulse rate and saturations, it is a particularly important diagnostic and monitoring tool. Nearly all patients in secondary care will be exposed to this device at some stage during their stay, often multiple times per day.

Why is there a problem?

A pulse oximeter will contact the fingers of multiple patients every day. If the pulse oximeter is not cleaned between uses this represents a serious clinical risk because humans have a natural tendency to put their potentially contaminated fingers into their mouth, so any pathogens present on the pulse oximeter are likely to be orally ingested by the patient.

Important pathogens spread via this route include coronavirus, norovirus, rotavirus, C.difficile, MRSA, E.coli, and others. A World Health Organisation's report for the Saves Lives: Clean Your Hands initiative (to which 26,875 hospitals are signatories) stated that HCAI spread "…occurs mostly via health-care workers' contaminated hands, contaminated items/equipment, and environment."

Why don't existing cleaning methods work?

Although best practice already requires cleaning after each use, existing methods are unsuited to the modern healthcare environment because they require healthcare staff to act outside existing workflow. Any task that is in addition to the basic provision of care can only be done in the quietest times and therefore cannot possibly be done consistently.

The limitations of existing methods mean that cleaning pulse oximeters is currently performed only sporadically, if at all. A better solution that works with healthcare staff is much needed.

Existing approaches to cleaning

  1. Do nothing

    Despite the evidence that contaminated pulse oximeters spread infection, many providers currently do not have an established cleaning program. This would seem to be a direct reflection of the unsuitability of present methods. The 'do nothing' approach has a very real and direct cost, both financial and clinical.

  2. Manual cleaning

    Manual cleaning with swabs is highly laborious, time-consuming, and impossible to ensure consistency from one clean to the next. Manual cleaning is readily neglected by overworked staff. A healthcare worker in an acute setting may need to clean a pulse oximeter upwards of fifty times per day.

  3. Disposables / single-use

    While single-use disposables provide an effective solution, they are expensive and environmentally unsound, require a flawless supply chain, and are reliant upon staff remembering to replace them. In many cases, staff in busy areas take 'short-cuts' into reusing them rather than seeking a replacement from storage.

Existing methods are unsuited to the modern healthcare environment. A better solution is required that works with healthcare staff, rather than against them.
